Art Visualisation Data

Data visualisation is the representation of data or big data in a way that is aesthetically pleasing to people, to attract people. The purpose of data visualisations is to attract and also help people understand complex data and to identify trends and patterns.

Data visualisation is useful because many people are attracted to the visuals rather than a lot of text, so it is perfect for people who want to learn but hate to read. And as such it is important that developers use techniques that will make their data look aesthetically pleasing and also make sense graphically. Kura, in his article titled Data Visualisation- An Underrated Art (2020) mentions that developers often make mistakes when it comes to how they represent their data visually, he also gives advice on why developers should follow data heuristics. For example, Kura (2020) advices that against a pie chart when creating data visualisations that show a change over time, he says that a bar chart would be a better suit instead.

In his article, Kura (2020) mentions the four rules of effective data visualisation coined by Edward Tufte:
  1. Data-Ink ratio

    2. This rule states that the “data-ink ratio is defined by amount of data-ink divided by the total ink required to print the graphic” (Kura, 2020). This means that when creating visualisations, developers should consider leaving out information that has no use in the visualisation, information such as they key of the graph etc. Kura says that by increasing the data-ink ratio, this will make the data visualisation more readable and easier to understand, bettering the user experience.

  2. Chart Junk

    3. There are two types of chart junk according to Edward Tufte; which are the unintended optical art and the grid. Unintended optical art occurs when there is “excessive shading and patterning of chart features”, these are not good because they can cause visual fatigue (Kura, 2020). The solution to this is labelling the chart features directly. The next chart junk is the grid; when creating a graph (especially bar graphs) the grid is unnecessary because it competes with the data being represented, and it ends up looking messy. The solution to this is making the grid lines lighter or removing them completely

  3. Spark lines

    4. Using Spark lines is encouraged to visualised data because they don’t need one to use axis, they are not complex, and the developers will spend less time visualising the data should they use them.

  4. Lie Factor

    5. The Lie Factor is “the size of an effect shown in the visual divided by the size of the effect actually in the data” (Kura, 2020). Kura(2020) mentions that this is done unintentionally for storytelling, however, it misleads the observers.

The lesson in this is that when making data visualisations, whether static or interactive, it is important to consider how you are representing the data; it is important for developers to use methods that will help their visualisations to be readable and accessible. Developers should be careful to not make their visualisations complex in the attempt to make them aesthetically pleasing to users.
